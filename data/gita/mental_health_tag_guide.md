# Spiritual Wellness Tagging Guide for Bhagavad Gita Verses

## Purpose

This guide provides a comprehensive methodology for tagging all 700 Bhagavad Gita verses with spiritual wellness applications, ensuring:
- Evidence-based alignment with modern psychology
- Cultural authenticity and respect for the original teachings
- Clinical relevance for the KIAAN ecosystem
- Consistency across all verse tagging

---

## Primary Domains

Each verse receives **one primary domain** representing its core spiritual wellness application.

### 1. anxiety - Worry, Fear of Outcomes

**Definition:**
Verses that address excessive worry about future outcomes, anticipatory anxiety, performance pressure, and fear of uncertainty.

**Psychological Basis:**
- Cognitive reframing of outcome-oriented thinking
- Present-moment awareness (mindfulness)
- Acceptance of uncertainty
- Letting go of excessive control

**Key Chapters:**
- Chapter 2: Karma Yoga foundations (verses 47-50)
- Chapter 6: Mind control and meditation (verses 35-36)
- Chapter 12: Devotional surrender (verses 6-12)

## Spiritual Wellness Applications

Applications are **specific interventions or therapeutic targets** that verses address. A verse can have multiple applications.

### Outcome Detachment

**Description:**
Reducing anxiety and stress by focusing on effort and process rather than results.

**Relevant Verses:**
- 2.47: Right to action, not fruits
- 2.48: Equanimity is yoga
- 12.12: Better to renounce fruits
- 18.66: Surrender all anxiety

**Clinical Context:**
- Performance anxiety (exams, interviews, competitions)
- Perfectionism
- Control issues
- Result-oriented stress

**Evidence Base:**
- Aligns with ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)
- Process goals vs. outcome goals research
- Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR)

**Sanskrit Terms:**
- **Nishkama Karma**: Desireless action
- **Phala Tyaga**: Renunciation of fruits

## Tagging Principles

### 1. Verse-Based, Not Interpretation

**Principle:**
Tag based on what the verse explicitly teaches, not on commentary or extended interpretation.

**Example:**
- ‚úÖ Verse 2.47 explicitly teaches outcome detachment ‚Üí Tag: `outcome_detachment`
- ‚ùå Don't tag verse 2.47 with `time_management` (that's an interpretation)

---

### 2. Multiple Applications Allowed

**Principle:**
Many verses address multiple spiritual wellness concerns. Tag with 3-5 applications when appropriate.

**Example - Verse 2.56:**
```
Applications:
- equanimity
- emotional_stability
- mindfulness
- stress_management
```

---

### 3. Primary + Secondary Domains

**Principle:**
- **One primary domain**: The core spiritual wellness area
- **0-3 secondary domains**: Additional relevant areas

**Example - Verse 6.5:**
```
Primary domain: self_worth
Secondary domains: [depression, purpose]
```

---

### 4. Evidence-Based

**Principle:**
Applications should align with recognized spiritual wellness interventions and have research support.

**Check:**
- Does this align with CBT, DBT, ACT, CFT, or other evidence-based therapies?
- Is there research supporting this application?
- Can this be explained in clinical terms?

---

### 5. Sanskrit Terms Preserved

**Principle:**
Key Sanskrit concepts should be preserved and explained, maintaining cultural authenticity.

**Important Terms:**
- **Sthitaprajna**: Steady wisdom (not just "calm person")
- **Nishkama Karma**: Desireless action (not just "don't care about results")
- **Viveka**: Discrimination, discernment
- **Vairagya**: Dispassion, detachment
- **Samatva**: Equanimity, evenness

---

### 6. Modern Context Bridging

**Principle:**
Connect ancient wisdom to contemporary challenges while respecting the original teaching.

**Examples:**
- Work stress ‚Üí Karma Yoga
- Performance anxiety ‚Üí Outcome detachment
- Social media comparison ‚Üí Svadharma (own path)
- Burnout ‚Üí Nishkama Karma

---

### 7. Cultural Sensitivity

**Principle:**
- Respect the spiritual and philosophical depth
- Don't reduce to mere "self-help" or "life hacks"
- Honor the sacred nature of the text
- Acknowledge the cultural context

## Implementation Workflow

### Step 1: Read the Verse
- Read Sanskrit (with translation if needed)
- Read transliteration
- Read English translation
- Understand the core teaching

### Step 2: Identify Primary Domain
Ask: "What is the **main** spiritual wellness concern this verse addresses?"
- If about worry/outcomes ‚Üí anxiety
- If about despair/motivation ‚Üí depression
- If about emotional balance ‚Üí emotional_regulation
- If about self-concept ‚Üí self_worth
- etc.

### Step 3: Identify Applications
Ask: "What specific spiritual wellness interventions does this support?"
Examples:
- Teaches outcome detachment? ‚Üí `outcome_detachment`
- Teaches emotional balance? ‚Üí `equanimity`
- Teaches self-control? ‚Üí `self_mastery`
- Teaches compassion? ‚Üí `compassion`

### Step 4: Add Secondary Domains
Ask: "What other spiritual wellness areas is this relevant to?"
Usually 0-3 secondary domains.

### Step 5: Validate
- Does the tagging make clinical sense?
- Is it evidence-based?
- Does it respect the original teaching?
- Would a therapist find this useful?
